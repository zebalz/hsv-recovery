What actually works.

No snake oil. Just the clinically proven treatments, suppression protocols, and symptom relief used by the community.

Antivirals

The standard of care for HSV suppression is valacyclovir (brand name Valtrex), taken daily as suppressive therapy. It is the most well-studied antiviral for this purpose, with a safety record spanning over two decades of widespread clinical use.

The clinical data is clear: daily suppressive therapy with valacyclovir reduces asymptomatic viral shedding by 70–80% and cuts overall transmission risk to uninfected partners by approximately 50%. When combined with consistent condom use, annual transmission risk drops to the 1–2% range. This is the protocol most infectious disease specialists recommend for sexually active HSV-positive individuals in serodiscordant relationships.

The standard suppressive dose is 500mg once daily. Some clinicians prescribe 1g once daily for patients with frequent recurrences or higher transmission concern. Both doses are available as generic valacyclovir, which costs roughly $15–30/month without insurance through most pharmacy discount programs.

Acyclovir (the older predecessor) and famciclovir are alternative antivirals with similar efficacy; valacyclovir is generally preferred for suppressive use because of its once-daily dosing and superior bioavailability.

Getting a prescription

For those who prefer not to discuss an HSV diagnosis in person, several telehealth platforms offer discreet consultations and same-day prescriptions sent directly to a local pharmacy or by mail. Wisp specializes specifically in sexual health and STI management and is widely recommended within the community for its straightforward intake process. Ro (Roman) and Nurx are broader telehealth platforms that also handle HSV prescriptions with similarly discreet delivery. All three operate in most US states and accept major insurance.

Acute Management

During an active flare, the priority is reducing discomfort, protecting the skin barrier, and shortening duration. The following are over-the-counter options that address those goals directly.

5% Lidocaine ointment provides immediate localized numbing of the affected mucocutaneous tissue. Applied topically, it is the most effective OTC option for acute pain relief during an outbreak — particularly useful for lesions that make urination or walking uncomfortable. It is available without a prescription at most pharmacies. Apply a thin layer to the affected area as needed; avoid contact with eyes or mucous membranes beyond the intended site.

Epsom salt (magnesium sulfate) baths are a long-standing community standard for the first 72 hours of a flare. Dissolve one to two cups in a shallow warm bath and soak for 10–15 minutes. The mechanism is largely osmotic — the saline environment draws fluid from active lesions, promotes drying, and reduces secondary bacterial contamination of broken skin. Use unscented, additive-free Epsom salt only.

Zinc oxide barrier cream — the same formulation used for diaper rash — applied between soaks creates a physical barrier over healing tissue, reduces friction from clothing, and has a mild drying effect on active lesions. It is inexpensive, widely available, and has a strong safety profile. Look for formulations with 15–40% zinc oxide concentration; avoid products with added fragrances or skin sensitizers.

Supplements

The supplement landscape for HSV is mixed at best. Two compounds — L-Lysine and Zinc — have enough clinical and community data to be worth understanding clearly.

L-Lysine is an essential amino acid that competes with arginine for cellular uptake. HSV replication is arginine-dependent; the theoretical basis for L-Lysine supplementation is that reducing available arginine slows viral replication. The clinical evidence is genuinely mixed: studies consistently show that doses under 1g/day are largely ineffective in isolation, particularly without a concurrent low-arginine diet (which means reducing nuts, seeds, chocolate, and legumes). However, doses in the 1g–3g/day range have shown subjective improvement in recurrence frequency and outbreak severity in a subset of study participants, even without dietary changes. The effect is not universal and does not approach the efficacy of antivirals — but the compound is low-risk, inexpensive, and high community adoption persists because a meaningful portion of users report benefit. If trying it, start at 1g/day and assess over 60–90 days.

Zinc has a better-established role in immune function broadly and some evidence for reducing recurrence frequency when taken at standard supplementary doses (15–30mg elemental zinc daily). The evidence is not definitive enough to make a clinical claim, but zinc is one of the more commonly cited supplements in community protocols for immune support between outbreaks. Zinc picolinate and zinc glycinate are the most bioavailable forms. Avoid exceeding 40mg elemental zinc daily without medical supervision, as high-dose zinc supplementation can interfere with copper absorption over time.

Neither supplement replaces antiviral therapy. They are best understood as adjunct support — tools that some people find useful in managing recurrence frequency and overall immune resilience, not as primary treatment.
